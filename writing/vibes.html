It's Vibes

July 12, 2026

The era that we're living in has been given a number of different titles. The information age; the digital age; the attention economy; (somewhat jokingly but I think appropriately) age of wonders; age of abundance; and, more recently, the age of AI are a few. I've made my own contribution to this name game, offering "age of infinites." All of these titles point to one specific aspect or another of an era perhaps more multifaceted than any other in history. There's one aspect that's left uncovered from the nouns and adjectives I've listed above, though. This aspect is the importance of the vibe. Vibes are ever more important culturally, but also impressively ever more important in other areas of society as social expectations revolve ever more around them. It seems only natural to throw in another era label that takes this into account. Just one more title. It's the vibes age, everyone.

The vibes age refers to how feel and/or emotional response have become central to what we like. It's as simple as that. The algorithm has been instrumental in bringing this about. With the advent of social media, individuals and corporations soon discovered that content with certain vibes captured the most attention. This realization was used to engineer both social media content and other work, creating a positive feedback loop in which the receptors of the vibe craved the vibe further.

The result is that everything and everyone is ascribed a vibe - and intentionally so. Observing this in marketing content and product design is what inspired me to come up with this essay's concept. The vast majority of tech-related marketing content (which I would argue displays the bleeding edge of the art) goes beyond demonstration and seeks instead to meet the viewer on an emotional or aesthetic level. You see this in trends such as the use of warm lighting and/or analog effects, chasing the vibe of nostalgia. See this video by The Browser Company, actually from way back in late 2024 (I didn't realize Dia has been around for this long).

In product design, you increasingly come across the concept of something that feels nice to use, rather than just getting you from point A to B. I can name a number of examples, but I find an especially clear one in the mission behind the bookmarking tool mymind, which is heavily focused around the experience of using their software.

This significance of the vibe in product design and marketing takes advantage of an overall significance of the vibe in greater society. The "attention economy" would not exist without swaths of social media users chasing a vibe of completeness. The explosion of the trendy spot revolves around the apparent vibe experienced by being at one of these locales (which may or may not be actually felt in person). The selection of workplaces, educational institutions, and places of residence are experience-based, as these societies cater to the crave. You even see the vibe in the way individual members of society themselves are viewed. Individuals are now frequently defined by a few characteristics they appear to display, forming an overall "brand" (or "vibe," you might say). Beyond even character, there's the looksmaxxing trend, which associates one's worth with the aesthetics of their physical form. (Okay, definitely not a new phenomenon. But its organization into what it is today would not have been possible without an already-established prominence of the vibe.)

Vibes are the bare minimum. The fact that everything is given a vibe by the outside observer means that one's output must make this easy to do. This becomes even more imperative as the age of vibes coincides with the age of AI. Millions are using image and language models daily to output creative work that effortlessly communicates a vibe.

Whether or not the vibe is well-received by you as an individual viewer doesn't matter as much as whether or not a vibe of some sort is immediately communicated. The essence of a perceptible vibe has become a filter controlling whether or not output is given a second look. Vibes have become so expected that output not giving off an easily received vibe is simply ignored. Meanwhile, output that builds trust and fosters repeat engagement requires a vibe that is resonant and unique.

Resonant and unique vibes are the moat. Presenting a vibe that enough people resonate with in one's work and one's person has become a necessity for success. There are case studies everywhere. YouTubers have built multimillion dollar media businesses based on their content, inherently dependent on communicating unique vibes. In a more recent example, the tech-adjacent podcast TBPN mastered the right vibe for their most relevant audience (high-earning people in tech), building industry respect before later shaking hands on a high-profile OpenAI acquisition. The film studio A24 has cultivated a loyal audience by making sure its output fits in with an overarching "A24 vibe." As I wrote above, institutions and entire cities have won people over with the vibe they undoubtedly strive to communicate. At this very moment, corporations are competing with each other based on vibes, figuring out how to fine tune the vibe they give off to attract more customers. Entire political races have been won when the vibe was resonant and lost when the vibe was off.

If you've been online or in tech-adjacent spaces (both virtual and real-life), chances are you've heard the term "taste" floated around more often than before. Used most sincerely, it has become a driving force for the direction of design; used most disingenuously, it has become yet another buzzword destined for decreased relevance in the not-so-distant future. I would make the potentially controversial argument that "taste" is often simply the concept of the vibe shrouded in the vibe of sophistication. This is not to be cynical, though! I, for one, love the vibe. I appreciate aesthetic consideration, and I think the importance of feel and emotion in what we choose will drive the things we interact with day to day closer to what's naturally human. This does, however, stand as yet another thing we must consider in branding ourselves. I don't pretend to know a magic formula for a "resonant" vibe, but I will seek to follow the oft-repeated (though not stale) advice to cultivate my output around what speaks to me. In this way, I am what I vibe with. I am the vibe. We all are.
